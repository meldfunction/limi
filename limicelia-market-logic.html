01 — Why market legibility

Limicelia's language is precise, earned, and intentional. It also requires shared context to land — context that most buyers encounter for the first time in the moment they're evaluating whether to hire you. Market legibility is not a dilution of the work. It is a membrane. It lets people through the door so the specialized language can do its actual work.

The caption gets people to the table.
The specialized language tells them whether they belong there.

The risk runs in both directions. Too legible and you attract the wrong clients — organizations that want a strategy deck, not accompaniment. Too specialized and you lose buyers who would be deeply aligned but don't yet have the vocabulary. The goal is legible enough to enter, distinct enough to self-select.

01

The buyer encounters a caption they recognize — "governance redesign," "AI adoption consulting," "culture change." They understand what category they're in. Activation energy drops.

02

They read the specialized language underneath. "Accompaniment, not consulting." "Structure produces behavior." "The practitioner is changed by the process." Something either resonates or doesn't.

03

The wrong clients self-select out. Organizations seeking quick fixes, frameworks to impose, or a consultant who will take ownership — they recognize this isn't that. This is a feature, not a loss.

04

The right clients lean in. The language names something they've been circling. They weren't sure this kind of partner existed. The diagnostic conversation becomes easy to say yes to.

05

The commons flywheel starts. Anchor clients — who often found you through legible language — fund full accompaniment for solidarity-economy organizations who already speak the language and are waiting for a capable partner.

Market legible means

The outside world can place you in a category they already have

Not dumbing down. Translating. A caption that uses the buyer's existing vocabulary to point at what you do — close enough that they don't bounce, honest enough that it doesn't mislead. Think: the subtitle on a book jacket that earns the full read.

Market legible does not mean

Replacing your language with theirs

The specialized language stays. It does the actual differentiation work, signals worldview, and begins the real relationship. The caption is the handshake. The specialized language is the conversation that follows. Both are necessary. Neither replaces the other.

02 — Sector analysis

Limicelia's four-tier commons model maps almost exactly onto four distinct market sectors — each with different legibility needs, decision-making structures, and relationships to the specialized language.

Tier 1 · Anchor

Tech Companies, B-Corps, Well-resourced For-profits

Impact-oriented tech firms, social enterprise scale-ups, mission-aligned corporations

Legibility need

Highest need for market-legible captions. Decision-makers speak ROI, change management, org effectiveness. The specialized language may be unfamiliar but resonates when encountered. Entry point is often AI Strategy or Culture/Leadership.

Tier 2 · Mission-Aligned

Foundations, Funder Networks, Established Nonprofits

Community foundations, intermediary funders, large-scale service nonprofits

Legibility need

Moderate legibility need. Familiar with org development vocabulary; may know Liberating Structures or Theory U. Respond well to hybrid framing. Entry points span all seven domains.

Tier 3 · Core Mission

Nonprofits, Public Agencies, Community Orgs

Direct service nonprofits, civic orgs, mid-size community anchors, public health agencies

Legibility need

Mixed. Some are deep in the practice traditions; others are not. Cost consciousness is high — the commons model needs explaining here more than anywhere. Governance, Community Engagement, and Conflict Resolution are frequent entry points.

Tier 4 · Solidarity

Worker Co-ops, Mutual Aid Networks, Movement Orgs

Platform cooperatives, housing co-ops, labor organizing networks, grassroots advocacy

Legibility need

Lowest need. Often already speak the language — may know Freire, Macy, Generative Somatics. The market-legible caption can feel reductive here. Specialized language is the relationship. These orgs don't need to be persuaded; they need to know the capacity exists.

03 — Buyer & beneficiary

In most Limicelia engagements, the person who signs the contract is not the person whose work will change most. This gap is not incidental — it shapes everything about how you communicate, who you build trust with first, and what success looks like when you leave.

The Buyer

Approves the engagement, holds budget authority, is accountable to a board or funder

  • Executive Director, CEO, or Co-founder
  • Board Chair or Governance Committee
  • Program Officer at a foundation
  • VP of People / Chief of Staff at anchor client
  • COO or Operations Director

Speaks: outcomes, risk reduction, investment justification, organizational health. Needs market-legible captions to bring the proposal to a board or leadership team. May need to translate what Limicelia does for skeptics without the background.

The Beneficiary

Experiences the transformation — work changes, relationships shift, capacity is built or restored

  • Frontline staff and program teams
  • Mid-level managers carrying institutional knowledge
  • Community members in participatory design processes
  • Worker-owners in co-op governance processes
  • The founding team navigating co-leadership tension

Speaks: what's actually broken, what the system produces, what they're carrying. Often holds the most accurate diagnosis. Limicelia's methodology explicitly prioritizes their voice — frontline before leadership, separate before joint.

The productive tension: The buyer approves based on what they can articulate to stakeholders (market-legible). The beneficiary determines whether the work actually lands (specialized). A successful engagement requires trust from both — which means your communication must serve both registers simultaneously. This is not a problem to solve. It is a condition to design for.

A third role exists in many engagements: the influencer — the person inside the organization who has encountered Limicelia's practice traditions and champions the engagement internally. Often a mid-level practitioner, an equity lead, a co-founder who isn't the primary ED. They speak the specialized language already and become internal translators. Identifying and supporting influencers early significantly increases engagement success.

05 — 2×2 service matrix

Two axes organize how Limicelia's seven service domains reach different markets. The X axis runs from institutional/commercial orientation to movement/solidarity orientation. The Y axis runs from crisis response (urgent, reactive) to proactive transformation (chosen, generative). Each quadrant has a distinct buyer profile, entry language, and engagement arc.

← Institutional / Commercial                                   Movement / Solidarity →

↑ Crisis response Proactive transformation ↓

Institutional · Crisis

The Burning Building

Urgent, high-stakes, budget exists, leadership is ready to act because something is already failing

Leadership & Culture Conflict Resolution Organizational Memory Governance (crisis mode)

Buyer: ED or Board in active crisis — post-scandal, leadership departure, funder threat, team collapse
Language: "Stabilization," "leadership transition support," "organizational recovery"
Entry type: Diagnostic → Transformation

Movement · Crisis

The Breaking Coalition

Urgency meets complexity — power differentials, political stakes, relationships that took years to build now fracturing

Conflict Resolution Coalition Building Governance (co-op) Community Engagement

Buyer: Network coordinators, co-op boards, movement org leadership in internal conflict
Language: Specialized directly — Deep Democracy, power-aware facilitation
Entry type: Often begins as Diagnostic, but urgency compresses the arc

Institutional · Proactive

The Strategic Investor

Things are working well enough — but leadership sees the transition coming and wants to build capacity before the crisis arrives

AI Strategy Governance Redesign Community Engagement Field Building Org Memory (succession)

Buyer: Anchor clients (B-Corps, tech), foundations, established nonprofits with budget and runway
Language: Market-legible captions most important here. ROI framing, risk mitigation, future-proofing
Entry type: Diagnostic → Transformation or Anchor Partner

Movement · Proactive

The Commons Builder

Solidarity-economy organizations building toward something — governance that reflects values, knowledge systems that survive transitions, fields that strengthen from within

Field Building Governance (participatory) AI North Star Org Memory Community Engagement

Buyer+Beneficiary: Worker-owners, mutual aid networks, movement orgs — often funded through commons pool
Language: Specialized directly. These are the mission made visible.
Entry type: Transformation or Anchor Partner. Referral-only through Liminal Web.

← Institutional / Commercial Movement / Solidarity →

The matrix reveals a strategic truth: Limicelia's revenue model runs horizontal (institutional anchor clients fund solidarity-economy work), while the methodology runs vertical (crisis-response engagements and proactive-transformation engagements require different pacing, not different values). Market legibility matters most in the bottom-left. Specialized language is sufficient — and preferable — in the top-right and bottom-right. The top-left requires both simultaneously: the buyer is in crisis and needs fast trust, which means legible captions first and specialized depth as soon as the diagnostic conversation opens.
