00 — What this document is and how to use it

What this document contains

This is Limicelia's working market intelligence reference — the strategic layer under the website, translation guide, and persona analysis.

It answers three questions that come up constantly in sales conversations, funder meetings, and internal planning: what does this kind of work typically cost? Where does the money actually come from? And who is making the decision to engage?


The document covers all four tiers of Limicelia's commons model — from anchor clients at full rate to solidarity-economy organizations fully funded by the commons pool. Every figure is sourced or flagged as an estimate with methodology shown. Sources are cited with URLs throughout so anyone reading this can verify, challenge, or update the data as it ages.

How to use it

  • 01Pricing conversations — use the tier tables to ground estimates. Show the source chips to clients who ask how you arrived at a number. The methodology note up front gives you language for "here's how this was built."
  • 02Funder outreach — the funding source map (§08) and AI spotlight (§09) are written to support grant applications and funder briefings. The source citations are directly usable in proposals.
  • 03Client onboarding — the commons flow (§10) and rationale cards (§11) help anchor clients understand why the rate is what it is, and why it includes a redistribution component that isn't overhead.
  • 04Internal planning — the engagement-type budget table (§07) gives a cross-sectional view for forecasting. The TODOs section flags where data is thin and where strategic development work is needed.
  • 05Message conversation mockups — three sample exchanges illustrate how the translation layer (market-legible caption → specialized language) works in real inquiry moments across platforms.

What this document is not

Not a pricing sheet. Not a script.

These are ranges, not quotes. Every engagement is custom; the diagnostic conversation is where actual pricing gets built. Use this document to show range and rationale — not to anchor a number prematurely. The right use is: "engagements like this typically run in this range, here's why, here's what affects it in your direction."

Data freshness

Market rates shift. Review annually.

Nonprofit consulting rates, foundation grant sizes, and AI consulting market comparables are all moving. The 2024 data cited here is the most current available as of this draft. Key sources to re-check annually: Nonprofit.ist Consultant Survey (fall each year), Instrumentl grant database, and Ford/Hewlett org effectiveness grant program pages.

Confidence levels

Three types of figures used.

Sourced — directly from published research or grant databases. High confidence.

Comparable — from market-rate consulting benchmarks for similar-depth work. Medium confidence.

Estimate — practitioner reconstruction from engagement hours × rate. Transparent methodology; review before using in formal proposals.

0A — Key terms explained plainly

Some of the language in this document is specialized. Here's what we mean when we use it — and why we use it instead of more common alternatives.

Core term

Tier

As in: Tier 1, Tier 2, Tier 3, Tier 4

A shorthand for who the client is and what budget reality they're operating in — not a ranking of importance or quality. Tier 1 clients are well-resourced and pay full rate. Tier 4 clients are solidarity-economy organizations (co-ops, mutual aid networks) that receive full accompaniment through the commons pool at no direct cost. The tiers exist to describe how the redistribution model works — revenue flows from higher tiers to make lower tiers possible — not to sort clients by value. A worker co-op in Tier 4 is not a lesser client; they're the mission made visible.

Plain language alternative: "client segment" or "budget category" — but those lose the commons logic. We use tier because it names the relationship between segments, not just the segments themselves.

Tier 1

Well-resourced commercial

Tech companies, B-Corps, impact-focused for-profits. Pay full rate. Surplus funds the pool.

Tier 2

Mission-aligned institutions

Foundations, established nonprofits, funder networks. Pay near-full rate, often via capacity grants.

Tier 3

Core mission organizations

Community nonprofits, public agencies, civic orgs. Grant-dependent; partial commons subsidy sometimes needed.

Tier 4

Solidarity economy

Worker co-ops, mutual aid, movement orgs. Fully funded by commons pool. Full depth, no financial barrier.

Practice

Accompaniment

Staying with an organization through a transformation process — not just diagnosing and delivering a report. The practitioner is present during implementation, not just planning. The container is relational; trust is the instrument, not the framework or methodology.

Accompaniment implies a commitment: we don't leave when it gets messy. We named the practice after the French verb accompagner — to go somewhere with someone, not to lead them there.

Not the same as: consulting (delivers a product), coaching (focused on one person's development), facilitation (holds a single meeting or process). Accompaniment includes all three but is none of them alone.

Finance

Commons Pool

Also: commons redistribution, the pool

The financial mechanism at the center of Limicelia's business model. Surplus above practitioners' base rate from Tier 1 and Tier 2 engagements flows into a shared pool. That pool funds full accompaniment for Tier 4 organizations who couldn't otherwise access this kind of support. It's not charity — it's structural redistribution. The anchor clients know this is happening; for B-Corps, it's a reportable impact.

Not the same as: pro bono (a gift from one party), sliding scale (the client pays less, the practitioner earns less). In the commons model, practitioners are paid the same rate for Tier 4 work — the funding comes from a different part of the ecosystem.

Network

Liminal Web

The practitioner network that Limicelia draws from and is embedded in. Not a contractor roster — a living ecosystem of people who share enough values, language, and methodology to work together fluidly. Named for the sense that transformative work happens in thresholds: between what was and what's becoming.

Practitioners enter through Starter Cultures and Intentional Society — trust is established by showing up over time, not by credentials. The network includes connections to Enspiral, Sociocracy For All, Greaterthan, the International Bateson Institute, and others.

Not the same as: a subcontractor bench, a professional association, a certification body. Access is relational, not transactional.

Engagement type

Diagnostic

The entry engagement — 3 months of honest listening before any prescription. The practitioner speaks to frontline staff before leadership, holds separate conversations before joint ones, and produces a clear-eyed picture of what's actually happening in the organization — including things leadership may not have named yet. The diagnostic doesn't produce a report to hand off. It produces shared understanding as a foundation for what comes next.

Not the same as: an organizational assessment (often top-down, produces a deliverable). The diagnostic is the beginning of a relationship, not a product.

Engagement type

Transformation Engagement

Also: the full arc

A 6-month engagement following the diagnostic. Includes deep listening, honest diagnosis, co-design of interventions, accompanied implementation, and a harvest — capturing what was learned and what the organization now holds. Covers one or more of Limicelia's seven service domains. The arc is not linear; it follows what the organization actually needs, not a predetermined curriculum.

Engagement type

Anchor Partner

A 12+ month sustained engagement where Limicelia is embedded in the organization's ongoing work. Not a project — a relationship. Used when organizations are navigating multi-year transitions (leadership succession, governance redesign, culture shift, AI adoption over time). The practitioner becomes a trusted presence in the organization's ecosystem, available to respond as situations develop.

Pricing is retainer-based: a monthly commitment rather than a project fee. This creates predictability for both sides and signals relational commitment, not transactional use.

Economy

Solidarity Economy

Organizations and enterprises that are structured around mutual benefit rather than profit extraction: worker-owned cooperatives, mutual aid networks, housing co-ops, credit unions, community land trusts, movement organizations. They are often under-resourced relative to their social impact. Limicelia's commons model exists specifically to make organizational transformation accessible to these organizations. Tier 4 exists because of them.

AI concept

Workload Creep

The documented phenomenon where AI tools make individual tasks easier — so more tasks get added, expectations rise, and cognitive load increases rather than decreasing. First named systematically by UC Berkeley researchers. Upwork's 2024 survey found 77% of workers said AI decreased their productivity when total workload was accounted for. It's the reason "AI adoption" without a North Star conversation produces burnout, not relief.

AI concept

AI North Star

The values and intentions conversation that should happen before AI tools are selected or deployed. What are we trying to become? What will we do with the capacity that AI creates? What won't we outsource? What does regenerative use look like for this organization specifically? Building the North Star is a 2–3 month process. It's the foundational step that most AI adoption frameworks skip — and the one that determines whether adoption is extractive or regenerative.

Practice

Harvest

The closing phase of an engagement — capturing, naming, and distributing what was learned and what changed. Not a final report. A harvest is designed with the organization: what knowledge now lives in people rather than documents? What practices have taken root? What should be made visible to the broader field? Under Limicelia's CC BY-SA 4.0 license, harvested insights are shared back to the commons as a matter of principle.

Model

Post-Growth

A business design principle: Limicelia defined "enough" before starting, rather than optimizing for growth. Surplus above the defined base rate becomes access for Tier 4 organizations — not expansion of infrastructure, marketing, or practitioner headcount. Post-growth doesn't mean stagnant; it means growth is governed by values, not market logic. The commons pool is the mechanism that makes this concrete rather than aspirational.

08 — Where the money comes from

Budget availability is only half the question. Which budget line the engagement draws from determines the internal advocate, approval process, timeline, and how the work is framed to stakeholders.

Tier 1 · Anchor Clients

Internal budget lines — no external grant required

  • People / Culture / DEI budget

    Most common entry. Sits alongside executive coaching, culture surveys, and engagement programs. Decision owner: Chief People Officer or VP People. Often delegated below board level up to $75K.

  • Organizational Effectiveness / Transformation

    For larger engagements. Board approval typically required above $100K. Framed as change management or leadership development investment. Often a multi-year line.

  • L&D / Leadership Development

    Particularly for AI Strategy engagements. Gallup's $1.9T disengagement cost figure and Upwork's 77% AI-productivity-decrease stat support the ROI framing directly.

  • CSR / Impact budget (B-Corps)

    For B-Corps, accompanying solidarity-economy partners can be framed as part of the social impact portfolio — making the commons contribution a feature. The redistribution story is a marketing asset for certified B-Corps.

Tier 2 · Mission-Aligned

Mix of internal operating funds and capacity grants

  • Organizational capacity grants (foundations)

    Ford Foundation, Hewlett Foundation, and Kresge offer org effectiveness grants $50K–$150K+ for larger nonprofits. New Profit Build Investments: up to $1.5M multi-year. Application timelines: 3–9 months. Competitive.

  • General operating reserves

    Established foundations and nonprofits with strong reserves can fund Diagnostic phases from operating funds. Board approval required. "Organizational infrastructure investment" framing helps.

  • Funder-sponsored capacity building

    Some funders sponsor capacity-building engagements for grantees directly — paying for Limicelia to accompany their portfolio organizations. Growing avenue, especially for AI adoption accompaniment work. Columbus Foundation's Center for Change Management is an early model.

Tier 3 · Core Mission

Grant-dependent; multi-source stacking required above $30K

  • Community foundation capacity grants

    Primary vehicle. Vermont CF: up to $5K. Sunflower Foundation: up to $25K. Rolling or annual cycles. Best fit for Diagnostic engagements where the grant covers most or all of the cost.

  • National org effectiveness grant programs

    RRF Foundation for Aging, Kauffman Foundation, and similar offer $25K–$150K for organizational capacity. Competitive; requires strong sustainability narrative. Turnaround 4–12 months.

  • Major donor or board bridge funding

    For orgs in crisis, a board member or major donor occasionally funds an engagement as a discrete gift. Faster than grants. Worth surfacing in diagnostic conversations when grant timelines are too slow for the urgency.

  • Government professional development funds

    Public agencies and some federally funded nonprofits have professional development and training budget lines. AI adoption and governance redesign can qualify. Procurement rules apply — may require RFP process. Build 60–90 days into timeline.

Tier 4 · Solidarity Economy

Commons pool redistribution — not grant-seeking

  • Commons pool (Tier 1/2 surplus)

    Structural mechanism: surplus above base rate from Tier 1 and 2 engagements flows to the commons pool. Pool funds Tier 4 at equivalent depth. No application required — access through the Liminal Web and practitioner relationships. See limicelia.org/the-model.html for full model.

  • Inquiring Systems Institute fiscal sponsorship

    As a 501(c)(3) project of ISI, Limicelia can receive grants and donations that directly capitalize the commons pool — opening foundation grants specifically for solidarity-economy capacity building.

  • Partial co-investment from the org itself

    Some co-ops and mutual aid networks have small fundraising capacity. Even $2K–$5K creates skin-in-the-game and honors mutuality. Limicelia's model supports this when possible — it's not a requirement, but it shifts the relationship.

09 — AI strategy budget spotlight

Accompaniment, not implementation

11 — Why these rates are defensible

Accompaniment at this depth is priced below executive coaching ($250–$400/hr) and above generalist nonprofit consulting ($100–$150/hr). That positioning is intentional and defensible on four grounds.

Rationale 01

The work requires rare practitioner capacity

Six wisdom traditions, eight social technologies, somatic grounding, six-phase arc fluency — and the ability to hold inner and outer work simultaneously. The practitioner is the instrument. Training that capacity takes years. The rate reflects scarcity, not overhead. This is not generalist consulting with a methodology bolted on.

Rationale 02

Engagements are long; the relationship is the container

6–12 month engagements at high relational depth cannot be diluted with junior staff or templated deliverables. McKinsey engagement managers bill $834/hr on much shorter arcs. Limicelia's blended rate is modest relative to depth and duration — not inflated relative to peer boutique firms.

Rationale 03

The commons obligation is built in

A portion of every Tier 1–2 engagement funds Tier 4 access. Anchor clients are not paying for a premium product only — they're participating in structural redistribution that is part of the value proposition. The rate is slightly higher than a purely bilateral engagement, but so is the meaning.

Rationale 04

The deliverable is capacity, not a report

Standard consulting delivers a deck. Limicelia leaves behind practitioner capacity distributed through the organization — the ability to navigate future transitions without external support. That is a different and more durable asset. It is harder to price and harder to commoditize. Which is appropriate — it's also why the wrong clients self-select out.

Limicelia 10:02am
A standard assessment interviews leadership and delivers a report. Scenario 02 · Microsoft Teams · B-Corp COO, AI Strategy, Tier 1

Microsoft Teams External Chat · Marcus W.
MW
Marcus W. · COO 2:07pm
We rolled out AI tools to our whole team 6 months ago. Productivity is up on paper but our People team is flagging real burnout and some folks feel like they're doing more, not less. I was told you might have a different angle on AI adoption than the usual "change management" playbook?
L
Limicelia 2:22pm
What you're describing has a name: workload creep. AI makes tasks easier, so people do more. Expectations rise to match new capacity. Net result: higher cognitive load even as individual task speed improves. UC Berkeley researchers documented this pattern, and Upwork's 2024 survey found 77% of workers said AI actually decreased their productivity when you account for total load.

What most AI adoption frameworks miss is the conversation that should happen before tool selection: why are we doing this, and what are we going to do with the capacity it creates? We call that the North Star conversation. It takes 2–3 months to build well, costs around $15K–$45K, and it's what prevents the exact situation you're describing from compounding.
MW
Marcus W. 2:35pm
That framing is exactly right. We're already past that conversation but I wonder if it's not too late to retroactively build the container. What would that look like and what's the budget range for the full arc?
L
Limicelia 2:51pm
Retroactive works — we'd call it Organizational Imagination facilitation. We start by mapping where AI is draining vs. creating capacity (that's 4–6 weeks of listening, frontline before leadership). Then we build the North Star with your team based on what they've actually experienced. Then we design boundary practices going forward.

Full arc at your organization's scale: roughly $80K–$175K over 9–12 months, structured as a monthly retainer. We'd probably want to do a shorter diagnostic first ($18K–$30K, 6–8 weeks) to make sure we're building the right thing. This can sit in your L&D or People budget — we can help you frame it for board approval if that's needed.
