Engagement

Housing advocacy nonprofit
Leadership transition

Transformation engagement
6 months · Mid-Atlantic
Anonymized with permission

An executive director of nine years was stepping down — by her own choice, but without a succession plan, a clear narrative, or a board that had processed what was actually ending. The organization named it as a "transition planning project." What it actually was: a founding myth coming apart, a board that had never governed without her, and a team that had learned to read her moods as organizational weather.

We didn't arrive with a succession framework. We arrived and stayed — through the board retreat that surfaced the real disagreements, through the ED's grief at leaving something she built, through the moment the incoming director realized the job description had nothing to do with the actual job. Six months later the transition was complete, the board had a functioning governance structure for the first time, and the organization had a written account of its own history that the new director could actually inherit.

Result: Clean transition. First governance charter. Organizational narrative documented. New director retained at 12 months.

— The six-phase arc

Every engagement follows this arc. It's not a methodology applied from outside — it's a way of listening that shapes what becomes possible at each stage.

01

Discovery

Not validating leadership's view of the situation. Hearing what's actually true. Mapping where energy is lost — and where it concentrates unexpectedly.

02

Deep Listening

Frontline workers before leadership. Separate before joint. Those closest to the problem hold the most accurate picture.

03

Honest Diagnosis

Naming what's actually broken — not softened. And what's working. Structure produces behavior, not just people.

04

Co-Design

The organization discovers its own path. The practitioner doesn't arrive with answers. Frontline and leadership redesign together.

05

Sustained Presence

Staying through the messy middle. Holding accountability to agreements. Navigating backsliding without collapse.

06

Harvest

What changed, what we carry forward. An internal memory document — not a funder report. Nutrients return to soil.

limicelia

limen — threshold, the space of crossing

mycelium — the underground network that connects and enables exchange between organisms

limicola — an organism that inhabits edges and margins

Our name is drawn from the biology of threshold crossings. The mycelium doesn't produce the growth — it connects, communicates, and creates conditions for what is trying to emerge. We believe organizations face genuine thresholds, not just problems: moments when old structures dissolve before new ones have formed.

A guide that can stay present at a threshold makes the difference between productive and destructive passage. That is what we are trained to do.

02 / 01

We follow the
organization's own knowing.

Every organization holds an intelligence about its own situation — often obscured, rarely absent. Our work begins with deep listening before any diagnosis, and continues by helping leadership access and trust what they already carry.

We do not arrive as experts on your organization. You are the expert. We are trained to help you hear yourself — and act from what you hear.

02 / 02

We name what is
actually happening.

Organizations in difficulty often name their pain as a strategy problem, a communication problem, a resource problem — because those framings feel manageable and safe to put in a document.

What gets covered: the unspoken power conflict, the founding myth that no longer fits, the grief of a transition, the ways a dominant narrative protects certain people from accountability. These difficulties live below the waterline — in patterns, structures, and mental models — and that is precisely where we are trained to work.

Naming what's real isn't harsh. Done well, it is an act of care — and the first condition of genuine change.

02 / 03

We work at the pace
transformation requires.

We do not accelerate processes that need time. Urgency is often the mechanism that produces the very fragility organizations are trying to escape — the rushed restructure, the quick-fix culture initiative, the leadership transition that skips the grief.

Our engagements are scoped to the arc of genuine transformation: 3 months to diagnose, 6 months to transform, 12+ months to anchor. We will tell you if a shorter engagement will serve you — and when it won't.

05 / Co-stewards

Two practitioners.
One shared commitment.

Limicelia is co-stewarded by BK and CX — a regenerative transformation practice working with mission-driven organizations navigating the gap between vision and reality. We create relational and imaginal experiences that help teams, organizations, and communities grow into what they are trying to become.

Our approach is rooted in living systems methodologies, contemplation and somatics, dynamic governance, and wisdom traditions. We don't prescribe solutions — we walk with organizations through creating them together, building containers and capacity for honest conversation, conflict navigation, and collective healing.

We believe consulting can be different and more alive when it returns to the basics of how natural systems learn, adapt, and create space for change. We have cultivated a partner network of organizations with similar values so we don't go it alone.
